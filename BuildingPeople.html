Building People

Individual Impact · Leadership

Leadership is not about managing designers. It’s about understanding people.

My belief

I don't believe people development happens through frameworks, templates, or performance reviews.

I believe it happens through human interaction.

Every designer is different.

  • Some need confidence.
  • Some need challenge.
  • Some need structure.
  • Some need freedom.

The biggest lesson I've learned leading teams of more than 29 designers is that one approach never works for everyone.

One shoe never fits all.

The moment you start treating everyone the same, you stop leading people and start managing resources.

My job has always been to understand how each individual thrives and then create an environment where they can do their best work.

Leadership is patience

Leadership looks glamorous from the outside.

In reality, it's patience.

  • It's having the same conversation multiple times.
  • It's helping someone through a difficult project.
  • It's creating space for people to make mistakes and learn from them.

The best leaders I've met weren't the loudest people in the room.

They were the ones who cared enough to invest their time in others.

Because growth doesn't happen overnight.

It happens through consistency.

Vulnerability matters

One thing I learned very early is that trust cannot exist without honesty.

People don't follow leaders because they have a title.

People follow leaders because they trust them.

I've always tried to be truthful with my teams.

  • When things are difficult, I say it.
  • When I make mistakes, I own them.
  • When something isn't working, we talk about it openly.

The moment leaders hide behind titles or politics, they create distance.

I prefer transparency.

Because transparency builds trust.

And trust builds great teams.

Attitude beats talent

I've worked with incredibly talented designers.

I've also worked with designers who weren't the strongest technically but became some of the highest performers I've ever managed.

The difference was attitude.

I care far less about qualifications than I do about mindset.

Skills can be taught. Tools can be learned. Processes can be improved.

Attitude is much harder to develop.

The people who succeed are usually the people who are curious, humble, proactive, and willing to learn.

Those are the people I invest in the most.

Protecting your people

A leader's responsibility is not only to deliver results.

It's to create an environment where people can sustainably produce their best work.

  • That means protecting focus.
  • Removing blockers.
  • Managing expectations.
  • Creating psychological safety.

The best teams don't perform because they're under pressure.

They perform because they feel supported.

People do extraordinary work when they know someone has their back.

What success looks like

For me, success has never been measured by the number of projects completed.

Success is seeing people grow.

  • Watching designers become confident.
  • Watching individuals step into leadership.
  • Watching people achieve things they didn't think they were capable of.

Products launch. Roadmaps change.

But helping someone grow their career lasts far longer than any feature you'll ever ship.

And that's the part of leadership I'm most proud of.
