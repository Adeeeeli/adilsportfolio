Accessibility & Workflow

Saturday, May 28, 2022

Let’s start with a summary of the basics.


There are multiple accessibility standards which all have the same goal: to make electronic and information technology accessible to people with disabilities.


According to GOV.UK, accessibility regulations came into force for the public sector bodies on 23rd September 2018. They say you must make your website or mobile application more accessible by making it ‘perceivable, operable, understandable and robust.’ You need to make sure your service meets level AA of the web content accessibility guidelines 2.2 (WCAG 2.2) as a minimum.


“by definition, there is an “affirmative duty,” to business owners to take the initiative and be in compliance.”


Digital Transformation


Ive been fortunate to work across multiple businesses outside of the public sector bodies, with compliance being an affirmative duty, meaning its up to the business to take on the work or not. Fortunately from my perspective the companies I have worked for, have been very receptive on making digital touch points meet the requirement of AA compliance.


The Early Days of Accessibility


At the very beginning of my career and with the early days of Sketch, il own up and say I must have missed the mark on compliance, but the dev team really had my back! I was questioned on accessibility during the design review stages even when it wasn’t a thing (before 2018). It wasn’t until learning front end development and actually managing content on EpiServer CMS did I really get an understanding of how important accessibility was.


I found myself writing alt tags on images and reorganising content on the page in EpiServer to make the web page more accessibale with the goal of trying to get into the good books with google search.


Additionally in Sketch when I was designing, I wouldn’t have a plugin and so I would manually export out colour codes, put it into an online accessibility checker to see if I was hitting AA compliance.


When I first got the brief to design an app with The Midcounties Co-operative I fell into a dilemma, the core brand guidelines didn’t meet the accessibility requirements. For context the brand itself is a house of brands instead of a branded house. So each subsidiary or product within the Co-operative portfolio would have its own brand guidelines… unfortunately for me yellow background and white text was engraved into the brand guidelines before my time so my job just got a lot more difficult.



I learnt so much about company politics, navigating and educating stakeholders and leadership. Creating almost pitch decks explaining how our core brand doesn’t meet accessibility and presenting it back to key stakeholders to get buy in. I was the sole UX/UI designer here and so this was definitely not an easy task by any means.


Eventually that boat sailed (positively I must add) and then came the understanding of screen readers and UI components built in a certain way. Whilst I knew this was part of accessibility I never quite understood its importance until a practical case of the app being used by an elderly user. They were fine using the app but the demographic audience required me to make the app even more accessible.


A TikTok video guide led me to turn on screen reader mode on my IOS device and wow did that shed a huge light on accessibility. All of a sudden error message states were on top of the form input field instead of below and return was added to the top of the mobile screen instead of the bottom.


I learnt so much about accessibility early on through various experiences and making mistakes. So if theres any UX/UI designers reading this just remember one thing:


Your audience can be anyone but one day someone with permanent, temporary or even situational disabilities will use your creations and the last thing you want is to make their life more difficult.


Make it stand out, Whatever it is, the way you tell your story online can make all the difference. Different type of disabilities: Touch, See, Hear, Speak | Permanent, Temporary or Situational



Dropping Accessibility into the workflow


Before I drop in a cheat sheet for you designers out there we need to talk about roles and responsibilities. Usually within a product launch you have some key players, each role has the responsibility of accessibility on the product and its about educating and ownership, its not all on the designer let me explain.


Product owners


You are the leaders of our organisation or product, this usually is our boss, manager, or product owner. Open the floor and make sure that accessibility is part of the house roles with resource to make it happen please.


Designers


You are the champion of the user, when designing the products make sure you hit all the points of accessibility and stay away from assumptions not everyone sees it the same way


Developers


For the most part you are the builders of the product but you’re also the ones with the most knowledge. Call it out, challenge and don’t be afraid to speak your mind. Follow best practices and use native code where possible … I wish people listened to you guys more.


QA Testers


That fine line between work in progress and launch, put your mouse aside and mash the keyboard, test the access use screen readers and incorporate it into the work flow.
